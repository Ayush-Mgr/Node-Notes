#  tangent slope and the limit
## Imagine a Curve 🎢


Say you have a curve y=f(x). You want the **slope at a single point** on that curve.

Problem:

- A _slope_ means rise/run between two points. ( rise = $\hat{y}-y$, run = $\hat{x}-x$)
    
- But here we want slope at **just one point** — sounds impossible, right?
    

---

## Step 1: Secant Line


Take two points on the curve:

- One fixed point P.
    
- Another nearby point Q.
    

Draw a line through them → this is the **[[2.  Function,Tangent, Secant, Chord, Diameter and Radius|secant line]]**.

- Its slope = f(x+Δx)−f(x)Δx.  
     $Δx = \hat{x}-x$
- It’s just “average change” between P and Q.
    
<img src  = "Unknown.png" style="filter: invert(1);">
---

## Step 2: Shrinking Q → P

Now, let Q slide closer and closer to P.

- The secant slope changes as Q moves.
    
- As Q _approaches_ P, the secant line starts looking like the **tangent line**.
    
<img src  = "Pasted image 20250831231807.png" style="filter: invert(1);">


---

## Step 3: Limit

That “approach” is exactly what **limit** means.

- The **limit of the secant slope as Q → P** = slope of tangent at P.
    
- That’s the **derivative**.
    

---

### Plain analogy 🏃‍♂️

Think of it like running:

- If you check your **average speed** between 2 points (home → bus stop), that’s like a secant slope.
    
- If you shrink the time interval smaller and smaller (1 minute, 1 second, 0.0001 sec), the **limit** gives your exact **instantaneous speed** — like looking at the speedometer.
    

---

So:

- **Secant slope** = average change.
    
- **Tangent slope** = instantaneous change. (The derivative)
	    the reason we called tangent line derivative is because it tells the next coming point how much it bends or is the slope from our current ones and that kind of shows the rate of change
	
- **Limit** = the bridge that turns average into instantaneous.

# find the area of a curve line


## Imagine a curve

Say we have a curve y=f(x), and we want the **area under the curve** between x=a and x=b.

At first glance:

- A straight rectangle area is easy (length × width).
    
- But the curve is _bendy_, so the shape isn’t a clean rectangle.
    

---

## Step 1: Chop into rectangles

Split the region into many thin vertical strips (rectangles).

- Each rectangle’s $height ≈ f(x).$
    
- Each rectangle’s width = small piece, say $Δx.$
    
- Rectangle’s area = f(x)⋅Δx.
    
<img src  = "Pasted image 20250831235819.png" style="filter: invert(1);">

---

## Step 2: Add them up (Riemann sum)



$$\text{Total area} ≈ f(x1)Δx+f(x2)Δx+f(x3)Δx+…$$

This is called a **Riemann sum** — basically a rough estimate using blocks.

<img src  = "Pasted image 20250831235819.png" style="filter: invert(1);">


---

## Step 3: Shrink the blocks



Now, make the rectangles thinner (Δx smaller).

- The approximation gets more accurate.
    
- As Δx→0, the sum → the **true area**.
    

This is the **limit** idea again.

<img src  = "Pasted image 20250831235912.png" style="filter: invert(1);">

---

## Step 4: Integral

That exact area in the limit is written as:

$$Area=∫_a^bf(x) dx$$

<img src  = "Pasted image 20250901000003.png" style="filter: invert(1);">
---

### Analogy 🍫

Imagine you’re filling chocolate under a curved shell:

- First, you fill with chunky blocks (rectangles).
    
- Then you use smaller and smaller chunks until it perfectly fills.
    
- The process of “infinite refinement” = the limit → the integral.
    

---

👉 So:

- **Secant → Tangent (via limit)** = _derivative_ (slope at a point).
    
- **Rectangles → True area (via limit)** = _integral_ (accumulated quantity).
