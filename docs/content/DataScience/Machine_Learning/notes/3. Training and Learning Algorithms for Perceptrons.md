### Training and Learning Algorithms for Perceptrons

Perceptrons are one of the earliest and simplest types of artificial neural networks, primarily used for binary classification problems. The **learning algorithm** for a perceptron involves adjusting weights based on input-output pairs to minimize classification errors. Here’s a detailed breakdown:

---

## **1. Overview of the Perceptron Model**

A **perceptron** takes multiple binary or real-valued inputs, applies weights, sums them, and passes the result through an activation function (typically the sign or step function):

$$y=sign(w⋅x+b)$$

Where:

- x is the input vector,
    
- w is the weight vector,
    
- b is the bias term,
    
- y is the predicted output (either +1 or -1 in binary classification).
    

---

## **2. Perceptron Learning Algorithm**

### **Goal**:

Find weight values w and bias b such that the perceptron classifies all training samples correctly (if linearly separable).

### **Algorithm Steps**:

1. **Initialize** weights w and bias b (usually small random numbers or zeros).
    
2. For each training sample $(x^i,y^i)$:
    
    - Compute the predicted output:
        
        $$y^i=sign(w⋅x^i+b)$$
    - Update the weights and bias if the prediction is incorrect:
        
        If $$y^i≠y^i:w←w+η⋅y^i⋅x^ib←b+η⋅y^i$$
    
    Here, η is the learning rate (a small positive constant, e.g., 0.01).
    
3. Repeat for multiple epochs (passes through the dataset) until convergence or a maximum number of iterations is reached.
    

---

## **3. Convergence Theorem**

- **Perceptron Convergence Theorem**: If the training data is **linearly separable**, the perceptron algorithm will converge in a finite number of steps to a solution that correctly classifies the data.
    
- If the data is **not linearly separable**, the perceptron will **not converge** and may oscillate.
    

---

## **4. Learning Rate η**

- Controls the size of the weight updates.
    
- Small η: slower learning but more stable.
    
- Large η: faster learning but can overshoot or diverge.
    

---

## **5. Limitations**

- Works only for linearly separable data.
    
- Cannot solve problems like XOR (non-linearly separable) without using multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs).
    
- Uses a step function, which is non-differentiable and unsuitable for gradient-based methods.
    

---

## **6. Example**
###  🎓 Perceptron Example — Predicting Pass Based on Study and Sleep Hours

We will train a **Single Layer Perceptron** to predict whether a student **passes** an exam based on:

- $x_1$: Hours of Study
- $x_2$: Hours of Sleep
- $y$: 1 if Pass, 0 if Fail

---

### 📊 Dataset

| Hours of Study ($x_1$) | Hours of Sleep ($x_2$) | Pass ($y$) |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------- | ---------- |
| 2                      | 0                      | 0          |
| 3                      | 6                      | 0          |
| 5                      | 8                      | 1          |
| 6                      | 7                      | 1          |

---

### ⚙️ Initialization

- Initial weights: $w_1 = 0$, $w_2 = 0$
- Initial bias: $b = 0$
- Learning rate: $\eta = 1$

---

### 🔁 Training (Epoch 1)

#### 🔹 Input: (2, 0), Target $y = 0$

$$
z = (0)(2) + (0)(0) + 0 = 0
$$
$$
\hat{y} = f(0) = 1 \quad \text{(Incorrect)}
$$
$$
\text{Error} = 0 - 1 = -1
$$

**Update**:
$$
w_1 := 0 + (-1)(2) = -2
$$
$$
w_2 := 0 + (-1)(0) = 0
$$
$$
b := 0 + (-1) = -1
$$

---

#### 🔹 Input: (3, 6), Target $y = 0$

$$
z = (-2)(3) + (0)(6) + (-1) = -6 + 0 - 1 = -7
$$
$$
\hat{y} = f(-7) = 0 \quad \text{(Correct)}
$$

✅ No update.

---

#### 🔹 Input: (5, 8), Target $y = 1$

$$
z = (-2)(5) + (0)(8) + (-1) = -10 - 1 = -11
$$
$$
\hat{y} = f(-11) = 0 \quad \text{(Incorrect)}
$$
$$
\text{Error} = 1 - 0 = 1
$$

**Update**:
$$
w_1 := -2 + (1)(5) = 3
$$
$$
w_2 := 0 + (1)(8) = 8
$$
$$
b := -1 + (1) = 0
$$

---

#### 🔹 Input: (6, 7), Target $y = 1$

$$
z = (3)(6) + (8)(7) + (0) = 18 + 56 = 74
$$
$$
\hat{y} = f(74) = 1 \quad \text{(Correct)}
$$

✅ No update.

---

### ✅ Final Weights After 1 Epoch

- $w_1 = 3$
- $w_2 = 8$
- $b = 0$

---

### 🧪 Test All Inputs with Final Parameters

| Input $(x_1, x_2)$ | $z$ Calculation                  | $\hat{y}$ | Correct? |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------- | --------- | -------- |
| (2, 0)             | $3(2) + 8(0) + 0 = 6$            | 1         | ❌        |
| (3, 6)             | $3(3) + 8(6) + 0 = 9 + 48 = 57$  | 1         | ❌        |
| (5, 8)             | $3(5) + 8(8) + 0 = 15 + 64 = 79$ | 1         | ✅        |
| (6, 7)             | $3(6) + 8(7) + 0 = 18 + 56 = 74$ | 1         | ✅        |

---

### 📌 Observations

- After 1 epoch, the model still misclassifies two inputs.
- More epochs are needed to fully converge.
- The perceptron is trying to find a **linear boundary** between pass/fail.

---

### 🧠 Key Formula Recap

$$
z = w_1x_1 + w_2x_2 + b
$$
$$
\hat{y} =
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } z \geq 0 \\
0 & \text{if } z < 0
\end{cases}
$$
$$
w_i := w_i + \eta \cdot (y - \hat{y}) \cdot x_i
$$
$$
b := b + \eta \cdot (y - \hat{y})
$$

---

## 🎯 Conclusion

A single-layer perceptron is a fundamental building block of neural networks. It’s simple but limited to linearly separable problems. It introduces the core concepts used in more complex networks like weights, bias, and activation functions.
