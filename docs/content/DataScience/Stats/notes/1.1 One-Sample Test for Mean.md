Used when you are comparing **one sample mean** to a **known or hypothesized population mean**
## 🧠 Introduction

**Hypothesis testing** helps determine if a sample provides enough evidence to support or reject a claim about a population **mean**.

You typically use it to test:

- Whether the population mean differs from a specific value.
- Whether two population means are significantly different.

---

## ✅ Choosing the Right Test

The test you use depends on:
- Sample size
- Whether population standard deviation is known
- Whether data is normally distributed

| Test Type         | Use When                                                                 |
|------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| One-Sample Z-Test | Large sample (**n ≥ 30**), **σ known**, normal or near-normal population |
| One-Sample t-Test | Small sample (**n < 30**), **σ unknown**, data must be normal            |

---

## 📐 One-Sample Z-Test for Mean

### When to Use:

- $n \geq 30$
- Population standard deviation $\sigma$ is known
- Normal distribution (or approximately normal)

### Hypotheses:

- Null Hypothesis $H_0: \mu = \mu_0$
- Alternative Hypothesis $H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0$ (two-tailed)  
  or $\mu > \mu_0$ / $\mu < \mu_0$ (one-tailed)

### Test Statistic:

$$
Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}
$$

Where:
- $\bar{X}$ = sample mean  
- $\mu_0$ = hypothesized population mean  
- $\sigma$ = population standard deviation  
- $n$ = sample size

### Example:

> A company claims the average weight of chocolate bars is 150g.  
> Sample of 40 bars:  
> $\bar{X} = 148g$, $\sigma = 4g$, $n = 40$  
> Test at $\alpha = 0.05$

#### Step 1: Compute Z

$$
Z = \frac{148 - 150}{4 / \sqrt{40}} = \frac{-2}{0.6325} \approx -3.16
$$

#### Step 2: Compare with critical value for two-tailed test at 5%  
$Z_{\text{crit}} \approx \pm1.96$

#### Conclusion:

Since $Z = -3.16 < -1.96$, **reject $H_0$**.  
There’s evidence that the mean weight is **not 150g**.

## 🧪 One-Sample t-Test for Mean

### When to Use:

- Population standard deviation $\sigma$ is **unknown**
- Sample size $n < 30$
- Data follows a normal distribution

### Hypotheses:

- $H_0: \mu = \mu_0$
- $H_1: \mu < \mu_0$, $\mu > \mu_0$, or $\mu \neq \mu_0$

### Test Statistic:

$$
t = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}}
$$

Where:
- $s$ = sample standard deviation  
- Other terms as above

### Example:

> A new diet claims to reduce cholesterol.  
> Sample of 10 patients:  
> $\bar{X} = 195$, $s = 10$, $\mu_0 = 200$, $n = 10$  
> Test at $\alpha = 0.05$

#### Step 1: Compute t

$$
t = \frac{195 - 200}{10 / \sqrt{10}} = \frac{-5}{3.16} \approx -1.58
$$

#### Step 2: Degrees of freedom = $n - 1 = 9$  
Critical value $t_{0.05, 9} \approx -1.833$ for one-tailed test

#### Conclusion:

Since $-1.58 > -1.833$, **fail to reject $H_0$**.  
There’s **not enough evidence** to say the diet lowers cholesterol.
