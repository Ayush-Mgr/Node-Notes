A "secondary company," as defined by Benjamin Graham in *The Intelligent Investor*, typically refers to smaller or less prominent firms compared to industry leaders or "primary companies." These are businesses that may not dominate their sectors or enjoy the same level of stability and visibility as primary companies. However, secondary companies often have potential for growth, albeit with higher associated risks.

Key characteristics defining secondary companies:
1. **Market Position**: They are not the dominant players in their industries.
2. **Financial Strength**: They may have weaker financial metrics compared to leading firms.
3. **Market Recognition**: They often receive less attention from analysts and investors, which can sometimes make them undervalued.
4. **Risk-Reward Profile**: Investments in such companies may carry higher risks but can potentially yield higher returns if they succeed.

Understanding these traits is crucial for investors evaluating whether these companies fit their risk tolerance and investment goals.
