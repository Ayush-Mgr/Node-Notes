A **joint distribution** describes the [[Probability distribution]] of two or more [[10. Random variable|random variables]] simultaneously. It specifies the likelihood that each possible combination of values for these variables occurs. 

### Key Concepts:

1. **Definition**:
   - If $X$ and $Y$ are two random variables, their joint probability distribution is denoted as $P(X = x, Y = y)$ (for discrete variables) or $f_{X,Y}(x, y)$ (for continuous variables).
   - It tells us the probability of $X$ taking the value $x$ *and* $Y$ taking the value $y$ at the same time.

2. **Discrete Random Variables**:
   - For discrete variables, the joint distribution is represented in a table or as a function:
     $$
     P(X = x, Y = y)
     $$
   - Example:
     For $X$ = outcome of a die and $Y$ = outcome of a coin toss:
     $$
     P(X = 3, Y = H) = \frac{1}{6}\times\frac{1}{2}=\frac{1}{12}
     $$
--- 
### Example (Discrete Case):

Let $X$ = outcome of a coin toss ($H=1, T=0$) and $Y$ = outcome of rolling a die.

| $X \backslash Y$ | 1    | 2    | 3    | 4    | 5    | 6    |
| ---------------- | ---- | ---- | ---- | ---- | ---- | ---- |
| $X = 1$ (Heads)  | 1/12 | 1/12 | 1/12 | 1/12 | 1/12 | 1/12 |
| $X = 0$ (Tails)  | 1/12 | 1/12 | 1/12 | 1/12 | 1/12 | 1/12 |

Here, the joint distribution gives $P(X = 1, Y = 3) = 1/12$, meaning the probability of flipping heads and rolling a 3 is $1/12$.
