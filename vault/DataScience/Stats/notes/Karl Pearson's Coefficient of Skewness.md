### **Formula:**

$$\text{Skewness} = \frac{3(\text{Mean} - \text{Median})}{\text{Standard Deviation}}​$$
1. in in symmetrical distribution, [[skewness#^7b3d16|mean median and mode are equal ]] 
2. larger asymmetry in data more discrepancy between mean median and mode . 
3. skewness is positive when mean is larger than median and mode and negative when is less.
4. So  the difference between them could be used to measure the skewness of data 
5. in Pearson's major this is measured in relative to standard deviation through  mean - mode if mode is not present than 3(mean - median)
### **Interpreting Karl Pearson's Coefficient of Skewness**

- measure lies between -1 and +1

- **Positive Skewness**:
    
    - If the coefficient is **greater than 0** (e.g., $\text{Skewness} > 0$), it indicates that the distribution has a longer tail on the right side, meaning the [[Mean]] is greater than the [[Median]]. This is often observed in datasets where there are a few exceptionally high values.
- **Negative Skewness**:
    
    - If the coefficient is **less than 0** (e.g., $\text{Skewness} < 0$), it indicates that the distribution has a longer tail on the left side, meaning the [[Mean]] is less than the [[Median]]. This is typical in datasets with a few exceptionally low values.
- **Zero Skewness**:
    
    - If the coefficient is **equal to 0** (e.g., $\text{Skewness} = 0$), it suggests that the distribution is symmetric, with the [[Mean]] and median being approximately equal
