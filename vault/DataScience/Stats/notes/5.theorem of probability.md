## 1. theorems oF total probability

^265b05

The **Law of Total Probability** is a fundamental principle in probability theory. It allows you to calculate the probability of an event by considering all the different ways that event can occur, typically through a partition of the sample space into disjoint subsets.



### Definition:
If $B_1, B_2, \dots, B_n$ are mutually exclusive and exhaustive events (a partition of the sample space), and $A$ is any event, the probability of $A$ can be expressed as:


$$
P(A) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A \cap B_i)
$$
simple way : $P(A)=P(A\cap B_1)...P(A\cap B_n)$
Using the conditional probability formula $P(A \cap B_i) = P(A | B_i) P(B_i)$, this becomes:

$$
P(A) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A | B_i) P(B_i)
$$or $P(A)=P(A | B_i) P(B_i)....P(A | B_n) P(B_n)$
---

### Key Terms:
1. **Mutually Exclusive Events:** Events that cannot occur simultaneously ($B_i \cap B_j = \emptyset$ for $i \neq j$).
2. **Exhaustive Events:** Events that together cover the entire sample space ($\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} B_i = S$).

---

### Explanation in Simple Terms:
The Law of Total Probability divides the probability of $A$ into "chunks," where each chunk represents the probability of $A$ happening under a specific condition ($B_i$). 

- $P(B_i)$: The likelihood of $B_i$ happening.
- $P(A | B_i)$: The likelihood of $A$ happening if $B_i$ has already happened.

You sum up these "weighted" probabilities across all possible $B_i$'s.

## 2. theorem of compound probability /Multiplication theorem 
- it states that probability of _occurring A as well as B_ = probability of a $\times$ by conditional probability of B assuming A or A has already occurred
- $P(AB)=P(A).P(B/A)$
- Where:
	- $P(A)$ is the probability of event A,
	- $P(B∣A)$ is the conditional probability of event B occurring, given that event A has already occurred.
- example, 
	- Finding the Probability of Getting Two Heads (HH) with Two Coin Tosses
		
		Let's apply the multiplication rule to find the probability of getting two heads (HH) when tossing a fair coin twice.
		
		1. **Step 1: Define the Events**
		
		- Event $A$: The first coin toss results in a head (H).
		- Event $B$: The second coin toss results in a head (H).
		
		We need to find $P(A \cap B)$, the probability of getting heads on both tosses, i.e., **HH**.
		
		2. **Step 2: Determine the Probabilities**
		   - Probability of getting a head on the first toss ($A$):  
		     $$
		     P(A) = \frac{1}{2}
		     $$
		   - Since the tosses are independent, the probability of getting a head on the second toss ($B$), given that the first toss was a head, is:  
		     $$
		     P(B \mid A) = \frac{1}{2}
		     $$
		
		3. **Step 3: Apply the Multiplication Rule**
		   Since the events are independent, we can multiply the probabilities of each event:
		   $$
		   P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}
		   $$
		
		 Conclusion:
		The probability of getting two heads (HH) when tossing a fair coin twice is $\frac{1}{4}$.
		
		This approach generalizes to multiple events. If you have three coin tosses, the probability of getting three heads (HHH) is:
		
		$$
		P(\text{HHH}) = P(\text{H on 1st toss}) \times P(\text{H on 2nd toss}) \times P(\text{H on 3rd toss}) = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right) \times \left(\frac{1}{2}\right) \times \left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{8}
		$$
### Independent Event 
- it is When occurrence of one event does not affect the occurrence of another.
	-  $P(AB)=P(A).P(B)$
	
- **Example 1: Tossing Two Coins**
	- Let $A$: First coin shows heads, and $B$: Second coin shows heads.
	- The tosses are independent, so:
	  $$
	  P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}
	  $$
	- Knowing the result of the first toss ($A$) does not affect the probability of the second toss ($B$):
	  $$
	  P(B \mid A) = P(B) = \frac{1}{2}
	  $$
	
	**Example 2: Rolling Two Dice**
	- Let $A$: The first die shows a 6, and $B$: The second die shows an odd number.
	- Rolling the dice are independent events, so:
	  $$
	  P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) = \frac{1}{6} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{12}
	  $$
	- Knowledge about one die's outcome doesn't affect the probability of the other's outcome.
	
	---
	
	### Key Takeaway:
	For events to be **independent**, their probabilities remain **unaffected** by any information about other events. Independence ensures no overlap in influence among the events, making calculations simpler.
