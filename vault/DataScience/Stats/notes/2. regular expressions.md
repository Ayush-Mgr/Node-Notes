# regular expressions (REGEX)
special sequence of characters that helps you find specific patterns in texts.


Let's look at two basic building blocks :

- **Literal Characters**: The most basic pattern is a simple character. For example, the regex `a` will find the 'a' in "apple". 2
    
- **The Wildcard (`.`)**: A period `.` is a special character that matches _any single character_. So, the regex `a.b` could match "acb", "a1b", or "a&b". 3 (_basically a any word b_)




__regex basic  table:__

| **Symbol** | **Meaning**                                   | **Example**                           |
| ---------- | --------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| `a`        | Matches character 'a'                         | `a` matches "apple"                   |
| `.`        | Matches any single character                  | `a.b` matches "acb", "a1b"            |
| `*`        | Matches **zero or more** occurrences          | `ab*` matches "a", "ab", "abb"        |
| `+`        | Matches **one or more** occurrences           | `ab+` matches "ab", "abb" but not "a" |
| `?`        | Matches **zero or one** occurrence            | `ab?` matches "a", "ab"               |
| `[ ]`      | Matches any **one character** inside brackets | `[abc]` matches "a", "b", or "c"      |
| `[^ ]`     | Matches any **character not** in brackets     | `[^abc]` matches "d", "e"             |
| ` or \|    | `                                             | Alternation (logical OR)              |
| `( )`      | Grouping                                      | `(ab)*` matches "abab"                |



## explanation

Here's an explanation for all the expressions from the table.

---

### `a` (Literal Character)

- **Meaning:** Matches the specific character itself. This applies to any letter (`a`, `b`, `C`, `D`, etc.), number (`1`, `5`, `9`, etc.), or symbol (like `_` or `%`).
    
- **Example:**
    
    - **Regex:** `cat`
        
    - **Matches:** "cat"
        
    - **Does not match:** "cut", "dog", "c a t"
        

---

### `.` (Dot or Wildcard)

- **Meaning:** Matches **any single character** (except usually a newline).
    
- **Example:**
    
    - **Regex:** `h.t`
        
    - **Matches:** "hot", "hat", "h8t", "h&t"
        
    - **Does not match:** "ht", "heat"
        

---

### `+` (Plus)

- **Meaning:** A quantifier that matches **one or more** occurrences of the preceding character.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - **Regex:** `ab+c`
        
    - **Meaning:** Match "a", followed by **at least one** "b", followed by "c".
        
- **Will Match:**
    
    - `"abc"` (one "b")
        
    - `"abbc"` (two "b"s)
        
    - `"abbbbbc"` (many "b"s)
        
- **Will NOT Match:**
    
    - `"ac"` (this has zero "b"s)
        

---

### `[ ]` (Character Set)

- **Meaning:** Matches **any single character** that is _inside_ the brackets. You can also specify ranges.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - **Regex:** `gr[ea]y`
        
    - **Matches:** "grey", "gray"
        
    - **Does not match:** "groy"
        
- **Range Example:**
    
    - **Regex:** `[0-9]` matches any single digit.
        
    - **Regex:** `[a-z]` matches any single lowercase letter.
        

---

### `[^ ]` (Negated Character Set)

- **Meaning:** The `^` (caret) as the _first_ character inside brackets means **NOT**. It matches **any single character** that is _not_ inside the brackets.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - **Regex:** `h[^o]t`
        
    - **Meaning:** Match "h", followed by any character that is **not** "o", followed by "t".
        
    - **Matches:** "hat", "h&t", "hit"
        
    - **Does not match:** "hot"
        

---

### `|` (Pipe or Alternation)

- **Meaning:** Acts like a logical **OR**. It matches the expression on its left _or_ the expression on its right.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - **Regex:** `cat|dog`
        
    - **Matches:** "cat", "dog"
        
    - **Does not match:** "mouse"
        

---

### `( )` (Grouping)

- **Meaning:** Groups parts of a regular expression together. This has two main purposes:
    
    1. To apply a quantifier (like `*`, `+`, `?`) to the entire group.
        
    2. To capture the part of the string that matched the group.
        
- **Example (for Quantifiers):**
    
    - **Regex:** `(ab)+`
        
    - **Meaning:** Match the _sequence_ "ab" one or more times.
        
    - **Matches:** "ab", "abab", "ababab"
        
    - **Without grouping,** `ab+` would mean "a" followed by one or more "b"s (e.g., "ab", "abb", "abbb").
    - 
## `*` VS    `?`

The main difference is the **number of times** they match the preceding character:

- `*` (**Asterisk**): Matches **zero or more** times.
    
- `?` (**Question Mark**): Matches **zero or one** time.
    

---

### In-Depth Look

Both `*` and `?` are **quantifiers** in regular expressions. They tell the regex engine how many times to match the character (or group) that came immediately before them.

#### `*` (Zero or More)

Think of `*` as "any number of this, including none at all."

- **Regex:** `ab*c`
    
- **Meaning:** Match "a", followed by **zero or more** "b"s, followed by "c".
    
- **Will Match:**
    
    - `"ac"` (zero "b"s)
        
    - `"abc"` (one "b")
        
    - `"abbc"` (two "b"s)
        
    - `"abbbbbbc"` (many "b"s)
        

#### `?` (Zero or One)

Think of `?` as "this is optional."

- **Regex:** `colou?r`
    
- **Meaning:** Match "c", "o", "l", "o", followed by **zero or one** "u", followed by "r".
    
- **Will Match:**
    
    - `"color"` (zero "u"s)
        
    - `"colour"` (one "u")
        
- **Will NOT Match:**
    
    - `"colouur"` (this has two "u"s, which is more than one)
        

### Side-by-Side Comparison

|**Regex**|**What it Matches**|*** Example: ab***|**? Example: ab?**|
|---|---|---|---|
|**"a"**|Matches (zero 'b's)|**Match**|**Match**|
|**"ab"**|Matches (one 'b')|**Match**|**Match**|
|**"abb"**|Matches (two 'b's)|**Match**|**No Match**|
|**"abbb"**|Matches (three 'b's)|**Match**|**No Match**|

##  `*` vs `()*`

Here's the difference:

The core difference is what the `*` (asterisk) applies to.

- In `an*`, the `*` only applies to the **`n`**.
    
- In `(an)*`, the `*` applies to the entire **`(an)` group**.
    

---

### `an*` (a followed by zero or more n's)

The `*` quantifier modifies _only_ the single character immediately before it, which is `n`.

- **Meaning:** Match "a", followed by **zero or more** "n"s.
    
- **Matches:**
    
    - `"a"` (zero "n"s)
        
    - `"an"` (one "n")
        
    - `"ann"` (two "n"s)
        
    - `"annnnn"` (many "n"s)
        

---

### `(an)*` (zero or more "an" groups)

The `( )` parentheses create a **group**. The `*` quantifier modifies the _entire group_ before it.

- **Meaning:** Match the _sequence_ "an" **zero or more** times.
    
- **Matches:**
    
    - `""` (an empty string, which is zero "an" groups)
        
    - `"an"` (one "an" group)
        
    - `"anan"` (two "an" groups)
        
    - `"ananan"` (three "an" groups)
        

---

### Side-by-Side Comparison

|**String**|**Matches an*?**|**Matches (an)*?**|**Explanation**|
|---|---|---|---|
|**"a"**|**Yes**|**Yes** (as `""`)|`an*` matches 'a' + zero 'n's. `(an)*` matches zero groups, finding an empty string match at the start.|
|**"an"**|**Yes**|**Yes**|`an*` matches 'a' + one 'n'. `(an)*` matches one 'an' group.|
|**"ann"**|**Yes**|**No**|`an*` matches 'a' + two 'n's. `(an)*` would need "anan".|
|**"anan"**|**No**|**Yes**|`an*` fails after "an". `(an)*` matches two 'an' groups.|
|**"annn"**|**Yes**|**No**|`an*` matches 'a' + three 'n's. `(an)*` fails.|
|**"ananan"**|**No**|**Yes**|`an*` fails after "an". `(an)*` matches three 'an' groups.|
