## 🧠 Purpose

This test checks if the **population variance** $\sigma^2$ is equal to a **hypothesized value** $\sigma_0^2$.

Common use cases:
- Quality control
- Verifying variance assumptions in modeling

---

## ✅ Assumptions

- The sample is drawn from a **normally distributed** population.
- The sample is **random and independent**.

---

## 📐 Hypotheses

- **Null Hypothesis** $H_0: \sigma^2 = \sigma_0^2$
- **Alternative Hypothesis** $H_1$:
  - $\sigma^2 \neq \sigma_0^2$ → two-tailed
  - $\sigma^2 > \sigma_0^2$ → right-tailed
  - $\sigma^2 < \sigma_0^2$ → left-tailed

---

## 🧮 Test Statistic

The Chi-square statistic is:

$$
\chi^2 = \frac{(n - 1) \cdot s^2}{\sigma_0^2}
$$

Where:
- $n$: sample size  
- $s^2$: sample variance  
- $\sigma_0^2$: hypothesized variance

---

## 🎯 Distribution

The test statistic follows a **Chi-square distribution** with:

$$
df = n - 1
$$

Use this to compare with the critical value from the _[[Chi-square - distribution table]]_ at the desired significance level $\alpha$ _(the columns aka. $X^2_\alpha$)._


---

## 📋 Example

> A factory claims the standard deviation of its ball bearings is 0.5 mm.  
> A sample of 25 bearings has a variance of 0.36 mm².  
> Test the claim at $\alpha = 0.05$

### Step 1: Parameters

- $n = 25$
- $s^2 = 0.36$
- $\sigma_0^2 = 0.5^2 = 0.25$

### Step 2: Compute $\chi^2$

$$
\chi^2 = \frac{(25 - 1)(0.36)}{0.25} = \frac{8.64}{0.25} = 34.56
$$

### Step 3: Decision

Compare with $\chi^2$ critical values at $df = 24$.  
If $\chi^2 = 34.56$ exceeds the upper critical value, **reject $H_0$**.

✅ There is significant evidence that the variance differs from 0.25 mm².
