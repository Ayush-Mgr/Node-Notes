## exhaustive set

^818ee6

- It is a group of all possible elementary events in a random experiment or situation 
- symbolically, $P(A\cup B)$ or  $P(A+B) = 1$
-  __Key Characteristics__
	1. **Completeness**: The set includes all possible outcomes.
	2. **Mutual Exclusivity (optional)**: The events are often mutually exclusive, meaning no two events can occur simultaneously. However, this is not strictly required unless specified.
- eg,
	- Rolling a Six-Sided Die
	  The sample space for rolling a die is $S={1,2,3,4,5,6}$.
	
	1. An exhaustive event set could be:
	
	- $B_1 = {1}$ (rolling a 1)
	- $B_2 = {2}$ (rolling a 2)
	- $B_3 = {3}$ (rolling a 3)
	- $B_4 = {4}$ (rolling a 4)
	- $B_5 = {5}$ (rolling a 5)
	- $B_6 = {6}$ (rolling a 6)
	
	
	 
	Together, $B_1 \cup B_2 \cup B_3 \cup B_4 \cup B_5 \cup B_6 = S = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}$is exhaustive.
	
	2. An exhaustive event set could also be:
	
	- Event $A$: Rolling an odd number $({1, 3, 5})$
	- Event $A_2$: Rolling an even number $({2, 4, 6})$
	
	Together, $A∪A_2=S$, so the set ${A,A_2}$ is exhaustive.

## independence
### Independent Event 
- it is When occurrence of one event does not affect the occurrence of another.
- symbolically,  $P(AB)=P(A).P(B)$
Let's break this down into simpler terms and explain step by step.

### What Does It Mean for Two Events to Be Independent?

Two events, **A** and **B**, are said to be **independent** if the occurrence of one event does not affect the likelihood of the other occurring. 

This means:
- The probability of **B** happening is the same whether or not **A** happens.
- Mathematically, this is written as:
  $$
  P(B) = P(B \mid A)
  $$
  where $$P(B \mid A)$$ is the probability of **B** occurring given that **A** has occurred.

Similarly, independence also means:
$$
P(A) = P(A \mid B)
$$

### Why Does This Work Both Ways?

Independence is a mutual relationship. If **B** is independent of **A**, then **A** is independent of **B**. Here's why:

1. **Using Conditional Probability Formula**:
   $$
   P(B \mid A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}
   $$
   If $$P(B \mid A) = P(B)$$, then:
   $$
   \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)} = P(B)
   $$
   Rearrange to get:
   $$
   P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)
   $$

2. **Check the Other Way**:
   If $$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$$, then:
   $$
   P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = P(A)
   $$
   So, if **B** is independent of **A**, then **A** is also independent of **B**.
