## Stop words
they are words with very less meaning and often occurs in a sentrences 
And are often removed during text processing.

such as :
- Articles: the, a, an
- ﻿﻿Conjunctions: and, but, or
- ﻿﻿Prepositions: on, in, at, by
- ﻿﻿Pronouns: he, she, it, they
- ﻿﻿Auxiliary Verbs: is, are, was, were, be
- ﻿﻿Others: this, that, there, then, so, very

__when not to remove some stop words :__

- **Sentiment Analysis** →  
    Words like _“not”_, _“never”_, _“no”_ can flip sentiment (e.g., “not good” ≠ “good”).
    
- **Text Summarization / Question Answering** →  
    Stop words such as _“what”_, _“why”_, _“how”_, _“when”_ help preserve question structure and meaning.
    
- **Machine Translation or Contextual Tasks** →  
    Removing them can distort grammar and sentence flow.
    
- **Named Entity Recognition (NER)** →  
    Some stop words (like “of” in “Bank of America”) are part of important names

---

__Advance way of removing :__

1. **[[10. Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging|POS]](Part of speech) Tag–Based Removal**

- Instead of blindly removing words, remove only those with **non-informative POS tags** like _determiners (DT)_, _prepositions (IN)_, _conjunctions (CC)_.
    
- Keep **adjectives, adverbs, and negations**, as they often carry meaning (esp. in sentiment tasks).  
    🧠 _Example:_ remove _“the”_, _“and”_, but keep _“not”_, _“very”_.
    

---

2. **Context-Aware Removal**

- Use the **semantic role or dependency parsing** to decide.
    
- Example: in _“not happy”_, “not” negates “happy,” so both are kept.
    
- In _“the bank of the river”_, “of” links meaningful nouns → maybe keep it.  
    📍Used in deep NLP pipelines and contextual embeddings (like BERT).
    

---

3. **Frequency Threshold**

- Remove words that appear **too frequently** (like “the”, “is”) across the entire corpus — they add little meaning.
    
- But you can also remove **very rare words** (noise) if they don’t help the model.  
    ⚙️ _Common range:_
    
    - Remove words with `freq > 0.8` (appear in >80% of docs)
        
    - Remove words with `freq < 0.01` (appear in <1% of docs)
        

---

4. **TF-IDF Filtering**

- stands for **Term Frequency - Inverse Document Frequency**.
	- **Term Frequency (TF):** How often does a word appear _in this one document_?
    
	- **Inverse Document Frequency (IDF):** How rare is this word _across all documents_?
- smart version of frequency threshold
- TF-IDF automatically down-weights high-frequency stop words.
    
- Instead of manual removal, rely on TF-IDF weights — words with **low TF-IDF scores** can be ignored.  

    ⚙️ Useful for: topic modeling, keyword extraction, and information retrieval.


| **Word**  | **Term Frequency (TF)** | **Inverse Document Frequency (IDF)** | **TF-IDF Score** | **Result**                             |     |
| --------- | ----------------------- | ------------------------------------ | ---------------- | -------------------------------------- | --- |
| **"the"** | High (in one doc)       | **Very Low** (in all docs)           | Very Low         | Gets a low score; seen as unimportant. |     |
| **"not"** | High (in one doc)       | **High** (it's not in _every_ doc)   | **High**         | Gets a high score; seen as important.  |     |

---

5. **Domain-Specific Stop Words**

- Create custom lists based on your corpus.
    
    - Example: in finance, words like _“company”_, _“market”_, _“share”_ may appear too often → treat them as stop words.  
        📍Domain-specific filtering improves relevance.
        

---


## **Categories of Stop Words**

### **1. Frequent Stop Words**

- **Definition:** Commonly occurring words that provide little meaning and are usually removed during preprocessing.
    
- **Examples:** _a, an, and, but, on, of, with, by_
    
- **Purpose:** Reduce noise by eliminating high-frequency, low-information words.
    

---

### **2. Domain-Specific Stop Words**

- **Definition:** Words frequently used within a specific field but not useful for analysis in that context.
    
- **Examples:**
    
    - Legal: _case, law, court_
        
    - Finance: _market, company, share_
        
- **Purpose:** Improve domain relevance by filtering repetitive, domain-common words.
    

---

### **3. Numeric Stop Words**

- **Definition:** Numbers or symbols that don’t contribute to text meaning (unless numbers themselves carry analytical value).
    
- **Examples:** _1, 2025, 100%, ₹, $_
    
- **Purpose:** Remove non-textual noise when analyzing semantics.
    

---

### **4. Single-Letter Stop Words**

- **Definition:** Individual letters that typically lack semantic meaning.
    
- **Examples:** _a, I, O, y_
    
- **Purpose:** Prevent meaningless single-character tokens from affecting models.
    

---

### **5. Context-Dependent Stop Words**

- **Definition:** Words that act as stop words in some contexts but are meaningful in others.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - _can_ → meaningless in general text,
        
    - but meaningful in _“a person **can** achieve it.”_
        
- **Purpose:** Enables **context-aware preprocessing** — preserving relevant words only where needed.
    

---

✅ **Summary Insight:**

> Stop-word handling isn’t one-size-fits-all — it depends on **frequency, domain, context, and task**.  
> Advanced NLP systems often combine **frequency-based**, **domain-specific**, and **context-aware** approaches for best results.
