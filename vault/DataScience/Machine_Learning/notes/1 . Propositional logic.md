# Propositional Logic

Propositional logic is a way of representing statements in a logical or structured form. It deals with propositions, which are statements that can either be **true** or **false**.

## Types of Propositional Logic

### 1. **Syntax**
Focuses on the formal structure of statements and how they are composed.

### 2. **Semantics**
Deals with the meaning and truth value of the statements.

---

## Propositions in Propositional Logic

### **1. Atomic Propositions**
These are simple statements with a clear truth value: either **true** or **false**.

**Examples:**
- "The sky is blue" → **True**  
- "1 + 2 = 4" → **False**

---

### **2. Complex Propositions**
These involve combining simpler propositions using logical operators. 

#### Types of Logical Operators:

1. **Negation (¬)**  
   Denotes the opposite of a statement.  
   **Example:**  
   - "Today is not Friday" (Negation of "Today is Friday").
   -  Negation simply flips the truth value of a proposition.  

| **P** | **¬P** |
|-------|--------|
| T     | F      |
| F     | T      |

---

2. **Disjunction (∨),{ like union}**  
   Represents an "OR" relationship between two statements.  
   **Example:**  
   - "You should eat or watch TV at a time."
   - Disjunction is **true** when at least one of the propositions is **true**.  

| **P** | **Q** | **P ∨ Q** |
|-------|-------|-----------|
| T     | T     | T         |
| T     | F     | T         |
| F     | T     | T         |
| F     | F     | F         |

---

3. **Conjunction (∧),{ like intersection }**  
   Represents an "AND" relationship between two statements.  
   **Example:**  
   - "Please like my video and subscribe to my channel."
   - Conjunction is **true** only when both propositions are **true**.  

| **P** | **Q** | **P ∧ Q** |
|-------|-------|-----------|
| T     | T     | T         |
| T     | F     | F         |
| F     | T     | F         |
| F     | F     | F         |

---

4. **IF-THEN (→)**  
   Represents an "IF-THEN" relationship between two statements.  
   **Example:**   ^5cf706
   - "If it rains, then the roads are wet."
   - Implication is **false** only when the first proposition is **true** and the second is **false**

| **P** | **Q** | **P → Q** |
|-------|-------|-----------|
| T     | T     | T         |
| T     | F     | F         |
| F     | T     | T         |
| F     | F     | T         |

---

5. **IF AND ONLY IF(IFF)  (↔)**  
   Represents "IF AND ONLY IF" (IFF) relationships.  
   **Example:**  
   - "I will go to the mall if and only if I have to do shopping."
   - Biconditional is **true** when both propositions have the same truth value (both true or both false).  

| **P** | **Q** | **P ↔ Q** |
|-------|-------|-----------|
| T     | T     | T         |
| T     | F     | F         |
| F     | T     | F         |
| F     | F     | T         |
