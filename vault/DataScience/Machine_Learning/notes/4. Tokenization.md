**Types of  tokenization**:


- sub-word tokenization: it converts words into  smaller meaningful pieces eg, playfulness -> `["play" , "ful" , "ness"]` 
---
- word tokenization : splits sentences into words eg, hi how you doing ? ->  `["hi", "how", "you", "doing", "?"]`
----
- _==sentence tokenization==_  (or segmentation): splits  splits paragraphs into sentences eg ,  hi how you doing ? , Iam doing pretty good. ->  `[" hi how you doing ?" , "Iam doing pretty good"]`

	__How It Work__
	1. Use punctuation and capitalization patterns:
		- Split at `.`, `?`, `!` followed by a space and a capital letter.  
		⚠️ But this can fail for abbreviations:
	 `“Dr. Smith is here.” → should **not** split after “Dr.”` 
	 2. **Statistical or ML-based methods**: 
	 - Models learn from data to detect sentence boundaries even when punctuation is tricky.  
		They consider features like:
		- Word patterns
		    
		- Capitalization
		    
		- Context before/after punctuation
		    
	
	 3. **Neural segmentation (modern approach)**:
	 - Transformer-based models (like spaCy, BERT-based sentence splitters) predict boundaries more accurately by **understanding context**, not just punctuation.

- ==_N-gram tokenization_== : 

 __What It Is__

**N-gram tokenization** means splitting text into **continuous sequences of _N_ items (tokens)** — where each item can be a **word**, **character**, or **subword**.
	
`“N” = number of tokens grouped together.`
	
	
 
 
 __Example (Word-level N-grams)__

Text:

> “I love data science”

|Type|Tokens|
|---|---|
|**Unigram (N=1)**|[“I”, “love”, “data”, “science”]|
|**Bigram (N=2)**|[“I love”, “love data”, “data science”]|
|**Trigram (N=3)**|[“I love data”, “love data science”]|



 __Why It’s Useful__

N-grams capture **local context** — relationships between neighboring words.

Example:

- “New” + “York” → “New York” = one concept
    
- Helps models understand short patterns like “not good” or “very happy”
    



 __Applications__

✅ **Language modeling:**  
Estimate the probability of a word given the previous N-1 words.

> P(“science” | “data”) ≈ how likely “science” follows “data”.

✅ **Text classification:**  
Use N-gram frequencies as features (e.g., in sentiment analysis).

✅ **Speech recognition & machine translation (classical models):**  
N-grams predict next words based on context → improves fluency.



 __Limitations__

⚠️ Doesn’t capture **long-range dependencies** (only nearby words).  
⚠️ Vocabulary grows fast with larger N.  
⚠️ Poor at handling unseen phrases (sparse data problem).

That’s why modern systems (like Transformers) **replaced N-grams** with **neural embeddings + attention**, which capture context more efficiently.
