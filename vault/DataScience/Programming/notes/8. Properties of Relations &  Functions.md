Here is a simple, clear note tailored for your exam review. I have used your examples and simple language to make it easy to memorize.

### **Quick Exam Note: Properties of Relations**

**Important Symbol Definitions:**

- **$A$** = The Set (The collection of items, e.g., $\{1, 2, 3\}$).
    
- **$R$** = The Relation (The pairs we picked, e.g., $\{(1,2), (2,1)\}$). **Note:** In this context, $R$ stands for **Relation**, NOT Real numbers.
    
- **$\forall$** = "For all" or "For every single one."
    
- **$\in$** = "Inside" or "Belongs to."
    
- **$\to$** = "Implies" (If this happens, then that happens).
    

---

### **1. Property and Symbol Table**

|**Property**|**Symbol / Notation**|**Simple Meaning**|
|---|---|---|
|**Reflexive**|$\forall a \in A, (a,a) \in R$|**The Mirror.** Every element pairs with itself.|
|**Irreflexive**|$\forall a \in A, (a,a) \notin R$|**No Mirrors.** No element pairs with itself.|
|**Symmetric**|$(a,b) \in R \to (b,a) \in R$|**Return Ticket.** If you go there, you must come back.|
|**Transitive**|$(a,b) \in R, (b,c) \in R \to (a,c) \in R$|**The Shortcut.** If $A \to B$ and $B \to C$, then $A \to C$.|
|**Antisymmetric**|$(a,b) \in R, (b,a) \in R \to a=b$|**One-Way (Loops OK).** No return trips allowed, unless you stayed home.|
|**Asymmetric**|$(a,b) \in R \to (b,a) \notin R$|**Strictly One-Way.** No return trips, NO loops.|

---

### **2. Detailed Explanations (Simple Words)**

#### **A. Reflexive (The "Selfie" Property)**

- **Definition:** For **every** element inside Set $A$, it must be paired with itself in $R$. You cannot miss anyone.
    
- **Example:** Set $A = \{1, 2, 3\}$.
    
    - $R = \{ (1,1), (2,2), (3,3), (1,2) \}$ $\to$ **Reflexive** (Everyone has a selfie).
        
    - $R = \{ (1,1), (2,2) \}$ $\to$ **Not Reflexive** (Where is 3,3? Missing!).
        

#### **B. Irreflexive (Opposite of Reflexive)**

- **Definition:** No element is allowed to pair with itself. If you see even one "1,1", it fails.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - $R = \{ (1,2), (2,3) \}$ $\to$ **Irreflexive** (No double numbers).
        
    - $R = \{ (1,2), (2,2) \}$ $\to$ **Not Irreflexive** (Because 2,2 is there).
        

#### **C. Symmetric (The "Two-Way Street")**

- **Definition:** For every pair $(a, b)$, you must find its partner $(b, a)$.
    
    - _Note:_ You do **not** need to check pairs like $(1,1)$ or $(2,2)$. They are their own partners.
        
- **Example:**
    
    - $R = \{ (1,2), (2,1), (3,3) \}$ $\to$ **Symmetric** (1,2 has 2,1. 3,3 is fine).
        
    - $R = \{ (1,2) \}$ $\to$ **Not Symmetric** (Missing 2,1).
        

#### **D. Non-Symmetric**

- **Definition:** This just means the relation is **not** symmetric. At least one pair is missing its return ticket.
    
- **Example:** $R = \{ (1,2), (2,1), (2,3) \}$.
    
    - Here, $(1,2)$ has a partner. But $(2,3)$ is lonely (missing 3,2). So the whole list is Non-Symmetric.
        

#### **E. Transitive (The "Shortcut")**

- **Definition:** If $a$ connects to $b$, and $b$ connects to $c$, then $a$ **must** connect directly to $c$.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - Check: We have $(1,2)$ and $(2,3)$. Do we have the shortcut $(1,3)$?
        
    - $R = \{ (1,2), (2,3), (1,3) \}$ $\to$ **Transitive**.
        
    - $R = \{ (1,2), (2,3) \}$ $\to$ **Not Transitive** (Missing the shortcut 1,3).
        

#### **F. Antisymmetric vs. Asymmetric (The Confusion Killer)**

This is where students make mistakes. Here is the simple difference:

1. **Antisymmetric:**
    
    - **Rule:** No return trips allowed... **EXCEPT** for loops like $(1,1)$.
        
    - _Traffic Rule:_ One-way street, but you can park in your own driveway.
        
    - **Example:** $\{(1,2), (3,4), (1,1)\}$ is Antisymmetric.
        
2. **Asymmetric:**
    
    - **Rule:** No return trips allowed... **AND NO LOOPS** allowed.
        
    - _Traffic Rule:_ Strictly one-way, and you cannot even stop at your own house.
        
    - **Example:** $\{(1,2), (3,4)\}$ is Asymmetric.
        
    - **Note:** If you add $(1,1)$ to this, it is no longer Asymmetric.
