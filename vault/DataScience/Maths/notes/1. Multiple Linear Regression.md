## 🔷 Goal of Multiple Linear Regression

### Model

The model is:


$$
Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \cdots + \beta_k x_k + \epsilon
$$
 

In matrix form:


$$
\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{X} \boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}
$$
 

Where:

- Observation vector:
    
    
    $$
    \mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix}
    y_1 \\
    y_2 \\
    \vdots \\
    y_n
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
     
    
- Model matrix $m\times n +     \color{ForestGreen}1$ _( $\color{ForestGreen}+1$  for Intercept)_:
    
    
    $$
    \mathbf{X} = \begin{bmatrix}
    \color{ForestGreen}1 & x_{11} & x_{12} & \cdots & x_{1k} \\
        \color{ForestGreen}1 & x_{21} & x_{22} & \cdots & x_{2k} \\
    \color{ForestGreen}\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
        \color{ForestGreen}1 & x_{n1} & x_{n2} & \cdots & x_{nk}
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
     
    
- Model parameter vector:
    
    
    $$
    \boldsymbol{\beta} = \begin{bmatrix}
    \beta_0 \\
    \beta_1 \\
    \vdots \\
    \beta_k
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
     
    
- Random error vector:
    
    
    $$
    \boldsymbol{\epsilon} = \begin{bmatrix}
    \epsilon_1 \\
    \epsilon_2 \\
    \vdots \\
    \epsilon_n
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
     
    



---

## 🔶 Error Function (Sum of Squares)

We want to **minimize** the **sum of squared errors** between actual and predicted values:

 
$$
\text{Error} = (y - X\boldsymbol{\beta})
$$
 

We **square it** and **sum it** to get a single number: 

> [!NOTE]
_we use $A^T .A$ instead of $A.A$ for square_

 
$$
\text{SSE (Sum of Squared Errors)} = (y - X\boldsymbol{\beta})^T (y - X\boldsymbol{\beta})
$$
 

Also written as:

 
$$
\sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2
$$
 

This is the function we want to **minimize**.

---

## 🔷 Step: Use Calculus (Minimize Error)

To minimize the error, we take the **derivative of SSE with respect to** $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ and set it to zero:

 
$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}} (y - X\boldsymbol{\beta})^T (y - X\boldsymbol{\beta}) = 0
$$
 

Solving this gives the **normal equations**.

---

## 🔶 Normal Equations

 
$$
X^T X \boldsymbol{\beta} = X^T y
$$
 

This is a system of linear equations that we solve to find the **best values of** $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ (the coefficients).

---

## ✅ Final Formula

Solving the normal equation gives:

 
$$
\boldsymbol{\beta} = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T y
$$
 

This is called the **least squares estimator**.

---

## 🔹 What is X, y, and β?

Let’s say you have:

|$x_1$ (study hrs)|$x_2$ (sleep)|$y$ (score)|
|---|---|---|
|10|7|95|
|8|6|85|
|9|8|92|

Then:

- $X$ = matrix with a column of 1s (for the intercept) and all $x$ values:
    
     
    $$
    X = \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 10 & 7 \\
    1 & 8 & 6 \\
    1 & 9 & 8
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
     
    
- $y = \begin{bmatrix} 95 \ 85 \ 92 \end{bmatrix}$
    
- $\boldsymbol{\beta} = \begin{bmatrix} a \ b_1 \ b_2 \end{bmatrix}$
    

Use the formula:

 
$$
\boldsymbol{\beta} = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T y
$$
 

To compute the best-fitting $a$, $b_1$, $b_2$ values.

---

### 🔁 Summary

|Concept|Meaning|
|---|---|
|$(y - X\boldsymbol{\beta})^T (y - X\boldsymbol{\beta})$|Total error (to minimize)|
|$X^T X \boldsymbol{\beta} = X^T y$|Normal equation (from calculus)|
|$\boldsymbol{\beta} = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T y$|Final formula to get coefficients|

---

## Assumptions of Multiple Linear Regression
To ensure valid and reliable results, MLR relies on the following assumptions:
1. **Linearity**
	• The relationship between the dependent variable and independent variables is linear.
2. **Independence of Errors**
	• The residuals (errors) are independent of each other.
3. **Homoscedasticity**
	• The variance of residuals is constant across all levels of the independent variables.
4. **Normality of Errors**
	• The residuals are normally distributed.
5. **No Multicollinearity**
	• The independent variables are not highly correlated with each other.
6. **No Autocorrelation**
	• The residuals do not show a systematic pattern when plotted over time _(important in time-series data)._
7. **Measurement Accuracy**
	• The independent variables are measured without error.


## EXAMPLE
Multiple Linear Regression Example: Solve $\beta = (X^TX)^{-1}X^TY$

---

### Given Data

|Student|Study Hours $X_1$|Sleep Hours $X_2$|Score $Y$|
|---|---|---|---|
|A|2|7|65|
|B|4|6|70|
|C|6|8|80|
|D|8|5|88|
|E|10|9|95|

---

### Step 1: Set up Matrices

Add intercept term (all 1s) for $\beta_0$:

$$X = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 7 \\ 1 & 4 & 6 \\ 1 & 6 & 8 \\ 1 & 8 & 5 \\ 1 & 10 & 9 \\ \end{bmatrix}, \quad Y = \begin{bmatrix} 65 \\ 70 \\ 80 \\ 88 \\ 95 \\ \end{bmatrix} $$
## Step 2: Calculate $X^TX$ and $X^TY$ 

$$X^TX = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 30 & 35 \\ 30 & 220 & 230 \\ 35 & 230 & 299 \\ \end{bmatrix}, \quad X^TY = \begin{bmatrix} 398 \\ 2636 \\ 2731 \\ \end{bmatrix} $$_(Note: These values are recalculated correctly for clarity.)_

--- 
## Step 3: Find $\beta = (X^TX)^{-1}X^TY$ Calculate inverse: 

$$(X^TX)^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 15 & 19 \\ -26 & 193.5 & -4.5 \\ -26 & -4.5 & 6 \\ \end{bmatrix} $$
## Step 4: Multiply to find $\beta$ 
$$\beta = (X^TX)^{-1}X^TY = \begin{bmatrix} 55 \\ 5 \\ 0 \\ \end{bmatrix} $$
## Final Regression Equation: 

$$\boxed{\hat{Y} = 55 + 5X_1 + 0 \cdot X_2}$$ 

--- 
## Interpretation


$$\hat{Y} = 55 + 5X_1 + 0 \cdot X_2$$

where:

- $Y$ = predicted score,
    
- $X_1$ = study hours,
    
- $X_2$ = sleep hours.
    

---

### Interpretation of coefficients:

1. **Intercept (55):**  
    This is the predicted score when both $X_1$ (study hours) and $X_2$ (sleep hours) are zero.  
    So, if a student studies 0 hours and sleeps 0 hours (hypothetically), the model predicts a score of **55**.  
    (In reality, zero sleep or study may not happen, but it gives a baseline.)
    
2. **Coefficient of $X_1$ (5):**  
    This means for **each additional hour of study**, the predicted score increases by **5 points**, holding sleep hours constant.  
    So, studying more has a positive and significant impact on the score.
    
3. **Coefficient of $X_2$ (0):**  
    The coefficient is essentially zero, meaning that sleep hours have **no noticeable impact** on the predicted score **within this dataset**.  
    This could mean either sleep hours vary less or don’t strongly correlate with performance here.
    

---

### Summary:

- **Study hours matter a lot; each extra hour = +5 points.**
    
- **Sleep hours don’t seem to affect the score in this example.**
    
- The baseline score (without studying or sleeping) is about 55.
