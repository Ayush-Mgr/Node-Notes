

### **1. Concept Overview**

In Data Science and CS, we can't just "guess" that an algorithm works. We have to **prove** it handles every edge case. A proof is a logical argument that establishes the truth of a theorem beyond any doubt.

### **2. Intuition (The Legal Trial)**

- **Direct Proof:** "Here is the murder weapon with fingerprints." (A $\to$ B $\to$ C $\to$ Verdict). Straight line.
    
- **Contrapositive:** "If the suspect were innocent ($p$), they would have been in Mexico ($q$). But they were NOT in Mexico ($\neg q$). Therefore, they are NOT innocent ($\neg p$)."
    
- **Contradiction:** "Let's assume the suspect is innocent. That would mean they were in two places at once. That's impossible. So, the assumption of innocence must be false."
    

### **3. The Three Main Techniques**

You are usually trying to prove a statement of the form: **If $P$, then $Q$** ($P \to Q$).

#### **A. Direct Proof**

- **Method:** Assume $P$ is true. Use algebra and logic to show $Q$ must be true.
    
- **Structure:** Assume $P$ $\to$ ... math ... $\to$ Therefore $Q$.
    

#### **B. Proof by Contrapositive**

- **Logic:** $P \to Q$ is logically equivalent to $\neg Q \to \neg P$.
    
- **Method:** Assume $Q$ is **False** (not Q). Show that this forces $P$ to be **False** (not P).
    
- **When to use:** When $P$ is messy but "Not $Q$" is simple (often with "odd/even" problems).
    

#### **C. Proof by Contradiction**

- **Logic:** Assume the **opposite** of what you want to prove (Assume $P$ is True AND $Q$ is False).
    
- **Method:** Show that this assumption leads to a logical impossibility (like $1 = 0$ or "even = odd").
    
- **Result:** Since the "opposite" is impossible, the original statement must be true.

### **4. Worked Examples**

**Prerequisites:**

- **Even number:** Can be written as $2k$ (where $k$ is an integer).
    
- **Odd number:** Can be written as $2k + 1$.
    

#### **Example 1: Direct Proof (Easy)**

- **Theorem:** If $n$ is an odd integer, then $n^2$ is odd.
    
- **Proof:**
    
    1. **Assume $P$:** Let $n$ be an odd integer.
        
    2. **Definition:** $n = 2k + 1$ for some integer $k$.
        
    3. Algebra:
        
        $$n^2 = (2k + 1)^2$$
        
        $$n^2 = 4k^2 + 4k + 1$$
        
    4. Factor: Look at the first two terms. We can pull out a 2.
        
        $$n^2 = 2(2k^2 + 2k) + 1$$
        
    5. **Conclusion:** Let $m = (2k^2 + 2k)$. Then $n^2 = 2m + 1$. This fits the definition of an odd number.
        
    6. **Q.E.D.** (Statement proven).
        

#### **Example 2: Proof by Contrapositive (Medium)**

- **Theorem:** If $3n + 2$ is an odd integer, then $n$ is an odd integer.
    
- **Analysis:** Starting with $3n+2 = 2k+1$ is hard because solving for $n$ gets fractions. Let's use Contrapositive.
    
- **Goal:** Prove "If $n$ is NOT odd (even), then $3n+2$ is NOT odd (even)." ($\neg Q \to \neg P$)
    
- **Proof:**
    
    1. **Assume $\neg Q$:** Let $n$ be even.
        
    2. **Definition:** $n = 2k$.
        
    3. Substitute into expression:
        
        $$3n + 2 = 3(2k) + 2$$
        
        $$3n + 2 = 6k + 2$$
        
    4. Factor:
        
        $$3n + 2 = 2(3k + 1)$$
        
    5. **Conclusion:** This is $2 \times \text{integer}$, which is clearly **Even**.
        
    6. Since assuming $n$ is even leads to $3n+2$ being even, the original statement is true.
        

#### **Example 3: Proof by Contradiction (Challenging - Classic Exam Question)**

- **Theorem:** $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.
    
- **Proof:**
    
    1. **Assume the opposite:** Assume $\sqrt{2}$ is **rational**.
        
    2. Definition: If it's rational, it can be written as a fraction $\frac{a}{b}$ in lowest terms (cannot be simplified further).
        
        $$\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}$$
        
    3. Square both sides:
        
        $$2 = \frac{a^2}{b^2} \implies 2b^2 = a^2$$
        
    4. **Inference 1:** Since $a^2 = 2 \times b^2$, $a^2$ is even. If $a^2$ is even, **$a$ must be even**.
        
    5. Substitution: Since $a$ is even, let $a = 2k$.
        
        $$2b^2 = (2k)^2$$
        
        $$2b^2 = 4k^2$$
        
        $$b^2 = 2k^2$$
        
    6. **Inference 2:** Since $b^2 = 2 \times k^2$, $b^2$ is even. Therefore **$b$ is even**.
        
    7. **The Contradiction:** We concluded $a$ is even AND $b$ is even. This means they share a common factor of 2.
        
    8. **Disaster:** This contradicts step 2 (that $\frac{a}{b}$ was in lowest terms).
        
    9. **Conclusion:** Our assumption was wrong. $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.

### **5. Common Mistakes**

- **Proving by Example:**
    
    - _Wrong:_ "$3$ is odd and $3^2=9$ is odd. $5$ is odd and $5^2=25$ is odd. Therefore it's true."
        
    - _Correction:_ Examples only demonstrate; they do not prove. You must use variables ($n$).
        
- **Assuming the Conclusion:**
    
    - _Wrong:_ Starting your algebra with the equation you are trying to prove ($n^2 = \text{odd}$) and working backwards. You must start with the premise ($n = \text{odd}$).
        

### **6. Data Science Connection**

- **Algorithm Complexity:** We use proofs to show that a binary search always finishes in $O(\log n)$ steps.
    
- **Model Consistency:** We prove that as we add more data, a machine learning model converges to the true answer (Consistency proof).
