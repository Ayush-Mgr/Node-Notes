Common Normalization technuiques are:

| **Technique**              | **Purpose / When Used**                                                            | **Key Note**                                                                                   |
| -------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Lowercasing**            | Makes text uniform and avoids treating _“Apple”_ and _“apple”_ as different words. | Essential for classical ML models; less crucial for transformer models (which are case-aware). |
| **Removing Punctuation**   | Cleans tokens like “hello!” → “hello”.                                             | Be careful in tasks like emotion/sentiment detection — punctuation can convey emotion (“!”).   |
| **Removing Numbers**       | Removes or replaces digits if numeric values are irrelevant.                       | In financial or scientific text, numbers are often meaningful → don’t remove.                  |
| **Removing Extra Spaces**  | Ensures clean tokenization and consistency.                                        | Always safe to apply.                                                                          |
| **Removing Stopwords**     | Reduces noise by removing high-frequency, low-information words.                   | Task-dependent (as we discussed earlier).                                                      |
| **Spelling Correction**    | Fixes typos to improve token matching.                                             | Use with caution — over-correction may change meaning (“no” vs “now”).                         |
| **Expanding Contractions** | Standardizes informal text (_“don’t” → “do not”_).                                 | Improves model understanding for written dialogues or social media data.                       |
| **Unicode Normalization**  | Handles accented/encoded text (_“café” → “cafe”_).                                 | Important in multilingual datasets or web-scraped text.                                        |

## Lemmatization
- slow but accurate and grammatically  as well 

converting words into their base dictionary form called lemma

lemmatization follows this steps :

 1. [[10. Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging|POS]] tagging: determining grammatical role of each words
 2. morphological: examine words structure to  get its root form
 3. dictionary:  referring dictionary to get words root form (lemma).

Example : 

1. **POS Tagging:** This is the "context" step. It figures out the word's job. Is "studies" a noun (He published two _studies_) or a verb (He _studies_ hard)?
    
2. **Morphological Analysis:** This is the "structure" step. It looks at the _form_ of the word. It sees "studies" is formed from a root plus a suffix ("-ies").
    
3. **Dictionary Lookup:** This is the "decision" step. It combines the first two steps:
    
    - "Okay, I need the lemma for the word 'studies'."
        
    - "Step 1 (POS) tells me it's being used as a **verb**."
        
    - "Step 2 (Morphology) tells me it ends in '-ies'."
        
    - "Step 3 (Dictionary) says the verb 'studies' has the lemma **'study'**."
        
Without Step 1, it wouldn't know if the lemma was "study" (the verb) or "study" (the noun). They happen to be the same here, but for "worse" (adjective), the lemma is "bad".

Example: lemmatization of word "worse"

- **Word:** "worse"
    
- **POS Tag:** Adjective
    
- **Lemma:** "bad"

__lemmatization methods:__

1. **Rule-Based Lemmatization:** Think of this as "smart stemming." It applies predefined linguistic rules, not just blind chopping. For example, it might have a rule that says "if a word ends in '-ing' and is a verb, remove '-ing' and add 'e' if the root is in the dictionary (like 'danc_ing_' → 'danc_e_')." 
    
2. **Dictionary-Based Lemmatization:** This is the most classic method. It uses a massive, pre-built dictionary that explicitly maps words to their base forms. The program looks up "worse" and the dictionary entry simply says: "lemma = bad." It's like a giant lookup table.
    
3. **Machine Learning-Based Lemmatization:** This is the most modern and complex approach. Instead of a fixed dictionary, a model is _trained_ on a huge dataset of text where humans have already labeled the correct lemmas. The model learns the complex patterns and contexts (like the POS tagging and dictionary steps, but all at once) to _predict_ the correct lemma, even for words it hasn't seen before.

## Stemming
- fast but not accurate and grammatically  as well 

Think of stemming as a quick, and somewhat "crude," way to chop words down.

It follows a set of simple rules to cut off the ends of words (the suffixes). The goal is to reduce different forms of a word to a common "stem."

**For example:**

- `connecting` might be chopped to `connect`
    
- `connected` might be chopped to `connect`
    
- `connection` might be chopped to `connect`
    

In this case, it works perfectly. All three variations are grouped under the single stem `connect`.

But because it just follows simple rules (like "remove 'ing'" or "remove 'ed'"), it can be messy.

**Consider these words:**

- `studies`
    
- `studying`
    

A simple stemmer might chop both of these down to `**studi**`.

Notice that `studi` isn't a real, dictionary word. Stemming doesn't care about that. It only cares about applying its rules to get a common root.

### How Stemmers Work (Porter, Snowball, Lancaster)

These are all different "algorithms" for stemming. They are essentially sets of rules for chopping off suffixes. The main difference between them is **how aggressive** they are.

- **Porter Stemmer:** This is one of the oldest, most common, and most gentle stemmers. It's designed for English and uses a series of rules to remove common suffixes. It's a good baseline.
    
    - _Example:_ `connection` → `connect` (Good)
        
    - _Example:_ `studies` → `studi` (The classic "messy" stem)
        
- **Snowball Stemmer (Porter2):** This is an evolution of the Porter stemmer (it was created by the same person). It's slightly more aggressive and more standardized. A big advantage is that it's not just for English; it has versions for many languages.
    
    - It often produces similar results to Porter but can be more effective.
        
- **Lancaster Stemmer:** This one is the **most aggressive** of the three. It's very fast, but it chops words down heavily. This can be good for some tasks, but it can also "over-stem" and reduce words so much that they become meaningless or different words get incorrectly merged into the same stem.
    
    - _Example:_ `humanity` → `human` (Porter)
        
    - _Example:_ `humanity` → `hum` (Lancaster - very aggressive!)
