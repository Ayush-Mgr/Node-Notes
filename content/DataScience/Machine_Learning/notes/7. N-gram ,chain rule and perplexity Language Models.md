    > it predicts next word in a sequence

### Basic language model 
A Language Model (LM) is just a system that assigns a probability to a piece of text. It answers the question: "How likely is this exact sequence of words?"

- P("I love NLP") → **High probability** (sounds like a normal sentence)
    
- P("NLP love I") → **Very low probability** (sounds like nonsense)
    

This is the core technology behind smartphone keyboard predictions, speech recognition, and machine translation


---

### The Chain Rule of Probability

to find the probability of a sentence $W$ (which is a sequence of words $w_1, w_2, ..., w_n$), we use the chain rule:

$$P(w_1, w_2, ..., w_n) = P(w_1) \times P(w_2|w_1) \times P(w_3|w_1, w_2) \times ... \times P(w_n|w_1, ..., w_{n-1})$$



Crystal-Clear Explanation:

In plain English, this formula says:

> "The probability of a whole sentence is the probability of the first word...
> 
> ...times the probability of the second word given the first word...
> 
> ...times the probability of the third word given the first two words...
> 
> ...all the way to the last word, given all the words that came before it."

The Problem This Creates:

Imagine trying to calculate the probability of the last word in a 30-word sentence. You'd need to calculate:

$P(\text{word}_{30} | \text{word}_1, \text{word}_2, ..., \text{word}_{29})$

This is impossible. You would _never_ find that exact 29-word sequence in your training data. This is the ultimate "data sparsity" problem.

How N-grams "Solve" This ([[The Markov Assumption]]):

This is where the N-gram approximation (also called the Markov Assumption) comes in3333. It's a "shortcut" that "solves" the chain rule's impossibility by making a big assumption:

- Instead of looking at _all_ previous words, we'll just look at the last $N-1$ words.
    
- **Bigram (N=2) Assumption:** We _approximate_ the "perfect" probability $P(w_3|w_1, w_2)$ with the _simpler_probability $P(w_3|w_2)$.
    
- **Trigram (N=3) Assumption:** We _approximate_ $P(w_4|w_1, w_2, w_3)$ with $P(w_4|w_2, w_3)$.
    

So, N-gram models are just a _practical, simplified way_ to estimate the true probability described by the Chain Rule.

###  How N-grams Work: The "Shortcut"

How do we calculate the probability of a whole sentence like "I love NLP"?

The _perfect_ way is to use the **Chain Rule of Probability**. This means we'd have to calculate: $$P(\text{"I"})×P(\text{"love"∣"I"})×P(\text{"NLP"∣"I love"})$$

**The Problem:** Calculating  $P(\text{"NLP"∣"I love"})$  is _hard_. You need to have seen the exact phrase "I love" many times in your data just to know what might come next. 
Now imagine predicting a word based on the 10 words before it!

**The N-gram Shortcut (Markov Assumption):** 
We take a shortcut. We _assume_ that the next word only depends on the last few N−1 words, not the entire history.

This gives us our different model types:

- **Unigram (N=1):** The "no context" model. We assume every word is independent.
    
    - To find P("I love NLP"), we just calculate: P("I")×P("love")×P("NLP")
        
- **Bigram (N=2):** The "1-word context" model. Each word only depends on the single word before it.
    
    - To find P("I love NLP"), we calculate:
	    $P(\text{"I"})×P(\text{"love"∣"I"})×P(\text{"NLP"∣"love"})$
        
- **Trigram (N=3):** The "2-word context" model. Each word depends on the two words before it.
    
    - To find P("I love NLP"), we calculate:
	    $P(\text{"I"})×P(\text{"love"∣"I"})×P(\text{"NLP"∣"I love"})$

###  calculating the probabilities

The method is called **Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)**, which is just a fancy way of saying "we count things from our training data"9999.

#### Bigram Example (The clearest one)

Let's find $P(\text{"am"} | \text{"I"})$ (the probability of "am" coming after "I").

The formula is simple:

$$P(w_i | w_{i-1}) = \frac{\text{Count}(w_{i-1}, w_i)}{\text{Count}(w_{i-1})}$$



So, we just count:

- How many times does the _sequence_ "I am" appear in our text? Let's say it's **50**.
    
- How many times does the _word_ "I" appear in total? Let's say it's **80**. (This is implied by the file's example $50/80$
    

The probability is just a fraction: $P(\text{"am"} | \text{"I"}) = \frac{50}{80}$.

#### Unigram Example (Even simpler)

Let's find $P(\text{"The"})$.

The formula is:

$$P(w) = \frac{\text{Count}(w)}{\text{Total Words}}$$


If our text has 1,000 words total and the word "The" appears 100 times:

$P(\text{"The"}) = \frac{100}{1000} = 0.1$.

This is the foundation of N-gram models.
#### Trigram

$$P(w_i | w_{i-1},w_{i-2}) = \frac{\text{Count}(w_{i-2},w_{i-1}, w_i)}{\text{Count}(w_{i-2},w_{i-1})}$$

## Perplexity

> Perplexity calculates the average number of choices for next word , 
>  low perplexity = less choices and high accuracy 
>  high perplexity = more choices, less accuracy

In simple terms, Perplexity is a metric that measures how  **surprised** your language model is when it sees a new, real sentence.,


- **Low Perplexity:** The model is not very surprised. The sentence looks familiar and probable. This is **good**.
    
- **High Perplexity:** The model is very surprised. The sentence seems weird and improbable. This is **bad**.
    

 **"Perplexity measures how well a model predicts a sequence"** . Lower perplexity means a better model.

### How is Perplexity Calculated?

The formula in your file looks a bit scary, but let's break it down.

The Formula:

$\text{Perplexity}(W) = P(w_1, ..., w_n)^{-\frac{1}{n}}$ 

**Crystal-Clear Explanation:**

1. **$P(w_1, ..., w_n)$:** First, you calculate the probability of your _entire_ test sentence (like "I love NLP") using your N-gram model (e.g., $0.00005$)4.
    
2. **$n$:** You count the number of words in that sentence (e.g., for "I love NLP", $n=3$)5.
    
3. **$^{-\frac{1}{n}}$:** This is the magic part. It does two things:
    
    - The **negative sign ($^{-...}$)** flips the probability. A small probability (like 0.00005) becomes a big number. This is how we get "high perplexity" for bad models.
        
    - The **$\frac{1}{n}$** part (the $n$-th root) _normalizes_ the score. It's like finding the "per-word" average surprise.
        

### The Example from Your File

Let's trace the calculation for the sentence "I love NLP":

1. **Model Probabilities:**
    
    - $P(\text{"I"}) = 0.1$
        
    - $P(\text{"love"} | \text{"I"}) = 0.05$
        
    - $P(\text{"NLP"} | \text{"love"}) = 0.01$ 7
        
2. **Total Probability:**
    
    - $P(\text{"I love NLP"}) = 0.1 \times 0.05 \times 0.01 = 0.00005$ 8
        
3. **Number of Words ($n$):**
    
    - $n = 3$ 
        
4. **Perplexity Calculation:**
    
    - $\text{Perplexity} = (0.00005)^{-\frac{1}{3}}$ 
        
    - $\text{Perplexity} \approx 27.14$ 11
        

The Key Interpretation:

Your file gives the best explanation: A perplexity of 27.14 means the model is "as uncertain as choosing between 27.14 equally likely next words".

Think of it this way: 
at each step, the model is trying to guess the next word. A perplexity of 27 means that, on average, it's like trying to guess the correct word from a list of 27 possibilities. A _really good_ model might have a perplexity of 10, 5, or even lower, meaning it's much more _certain_ about what the next word will be.
