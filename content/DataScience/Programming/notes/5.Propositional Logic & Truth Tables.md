### **3. Logical Connectives (The Syntax)**

### **4. Truth Tables (The Calculator of Logic)**

A Truth Table shows every possible scenario. Since you have an exam, memorize theô **Implication ($p \to q$)**ô table perfectlyãit is the most common source of mistakes.

**The Implication ($p \to q$)**

- Think of it as a promise: "If you score 100% ($p$), I will buy you a car ($q$)."
    

| **p (Score 100%)** | **q (Buy Car)** | **pãq (Did I lie?)** | **Reasoning**                                                     |
| ------------------ | --------------- | -------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| T                  | T               | **T**                | You scored 100%, I bought the car. Kept promise.                  |
| T                  | F               | **F**                | You scored 100%, I didô **NOT**ô buy car.ô **I LIED.**               |
| F                  | T               | **T**                | You didn't score 100%, but I bought it anyway. No promise broken. |
| F                  | F               | **T**                | You didn't score 100%, I didn't buy car. No promise broken.       |

> **Critical Rule:**ô $p \to q$ô isô **False ONLY**ô whenô $p$ô is True andô $q$ô is False. In all other cases, it is True.

### **5. Worked Examples**

**Example 1: Converting English to Logic (Easy)**

- **Sentence:**ô "If it rains ($p$), then I will stay home ($q$) or I will take an umbrella ($r$)."
    
- **Logic:**ô $p \to (q \lor r)$

**Example 3: Logic Puzzle (Challenging)**

- **Scenario:**ô "You cannot ride the roller coaster ($r$) unless you are over 5 feet tall ($h$) and older than 12 ($a$)."
    
- **Translation:**
    
    - "Unless" usually means "If not... then not".
        
    - "If you are NOT (tall AND old), then NOT ride."
        
    - $\neg (h \land a) \to \neg r$
        
    - _Alternatively via Contrapositive:_ô $r \to (h \land a)$ô ("If you ride, you must be tall and old").
        

### **6. Common Mistakes**

- **Confusingô $\lor$ô (Inclusive OR) withô $\oplus$ô (Exclusive OR):**
    
    - In English, "Coffee or Tea?" usually implies one or the other ($\oplus$).
        
    - In Math/Logic, "Or" ($\lor$) allowsô **both**.
        
- **Misunderstanding Implication ($p \to q$):**
    
    - Students often think ifô $p$ô is False, the whole thing is False.
        
    - **Correction:**ô Ifô $p$ô is False (False Hypothesis), the implication is automaticallyô **True**ô (vacuously true).
