## 🧠 Purpose

The F-test is used to compare the **variances of two independent samples** to determine if they come from populations with **equal variances**.

Common applications:
- Testing homogeneity of variance
- Validating assumptions before using parametric tests (e.g. t-tests)

---

## ✅ Assumptions

- Both samples are **independent**.
- Both populations are **normally distributed**.
- The data is **continuous**.

---

## 📐 Hypotheses

- **Null Hypothesis** $H_0: \sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2$
- **Alternative Hypothesis** $H_1$:
  - $\sigma_1^2 \neq \sigma_2^2$ (two-tailed)
  - $\sigma_1^2 > \sigma_2^2$ or $\sigma_1^2 < \sigma_2^2$ (one-tailed)

---

## 🧮 Test Statistic

The F-statistic is calculated as:

$$
F = \frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}
$$

Where:
- $s_1^2$: variance of the first sample (larger variance usually goes in the numerator)
- $s_2^2$: variance of the second sample

> ⚠️ Always put the **larger** sample variance in the numerator to ensure $F \geq 1$

---

## 🎯 Distribution

- The test statistic follows an **F-distribution** with:
  
$$
df_1 = n_1 - 1, \quad df_2 = n_2 - 1
$$

- Use F-tables or software to find critical values at the desired significance level $\alpha$.

---

## 📋 Example

> Sample A: $s_1^2 = 25$, $n_1 = 16$  
> Sample B: $s_2^2 = 10$, $n_2 = 21$

### Step 1: Compute $F$

$$
F = \frac{25}{10} = 2.5
$$

### Step 2: Degrees of Freedom

- $df_1 = 16 - 1 = 15$
- $df_2 = 21 - 1 = 20$

### Step 3: Conclusion

Compare $F = 2.5$ with the critical value from the F-distribution table for $df_1 = 15$, $df_2 = 20$, and your significance level (e.g., $\alpha = 0.05$).

If $F$ exceeds the critical value, **reject $H_0$** — variances are significantly different.

$\therefore$  $F <F_{crit}$ , we felt to reject $H_0$
